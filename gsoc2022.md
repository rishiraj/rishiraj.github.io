    <h2><span class="icon">üìù</span> Project Summary</h2>
    <p>Libraries like <a href="https://xgboost.readthedocs.io/">XGBoost</a> and <a href="https://lightgbm.readthedocs.io/">LightGBM</a> dominated most Kaggle competitions on tabular data and achieved state-of-the-art results on a variety of datasets. The <a href="https://www.tensorflow.org/decision_forests">TensorFlow Decision Forests</a> was also a very powerful new library with potential to give highly accurate predictions. However due to the lack of proper awareness and availability of detailed examples in the Kaggle community, it was not being used to its true potential. I solved this with a series of notebooks that demonstrated the usage of TensorFlow Decision Forests for a variety of datasets. I created beginner-friendly baseline examples and also dived into fine-tuned advanced examples that achieved some of the best scores in tabular competitions. As additional contribution, I‚Äôm working on a project that auto-trains, auto-tunes and auto-serves the best TF-DF model directly from CSV files.</p>

    <hr class="section-divider">

    <h2><span class="icon">üõ†Ô∏è</span> Contributions</h2>
    <p>To demonstrate getting started and automated hyper-parameter tuning in TF-DF I worked on a real Kaggle competition with the <a href="https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/tabular-playground-series-feb-2021">Tabular Playground Series - Feb 2021</a> dataset. It is a tabular dataset with 300,000 rows and 26 columns in training (93.66 MiB .CSV training dataset + 58.85 MiB .CSV test set) that is suitable for training algorithms to solve regression problems. The feature columns, cat0 - cat9 are categorical, and the feature columns cont0 - cont13 are continuous. I chose a regression task as the official tutorial already had examples for classification tasks.</p>
